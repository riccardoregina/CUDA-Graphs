\begin{abstract}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\noindent
We present a replication study and performance validation of the kernel batching strategy utilizing CUDA Graphs, originally proposed to optimize iterative high-performance computing (HPC) applications. By grouping multiple kernel launches into a single executable graph, the traditional host-side dispatch overhead is significantly mitigated. We evaluate the proposed unrolling strategy and its theoretical performance model across three distinct GPU architectures under varying workload intensities and mini-batch sizes. Our findings strongly corroborate the qualitative aspects of the original model, including the linear growth of graph creation costs and the presence of a ``U-shaped'' execution time curve that yields an optimal batch size between 50 and 100 nodes for light workloads. However, we observe notable quantitative deviations: our implementations exhibited a substantially higher performance speed-up (up to 2.5x, compared to the previously reported 1.5x) for light workloads, and a proportionally smaller memory footprint for the instantiated graphs. These results underscore that the tangible benefits of CUDA Graphs are highly dependent on the underlying hardware environment, where systems with higher host-to-device latencies can experience significantly greater relative performance gains.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------
The goal of this work is to replicate and validate the findings of `Boosting Performance of Iterative Applications on GPUs: Kernel Batching with CUDA Graphs' \cite{10974864} presented during \emph{2025 33rd Euromicro International Conference on Parallel, Distributed, and Network-Based Processing (PDP)}.

Reproducing published research is a foundational pillar of the scientific method that extends far beyond merely checking for errors. 
Even within rigorously peer-reviewed literature, independent replication is essential to verify the robustness and generalizability of proposed methods, ensuring that reported outcomes hold true across varying architectures, software environments, and edge cases. 
%The rigorous process of reproduction often uncovers undocumented implementation details or implicit assumptions that are crucial for practical application, thereby bridging the gap between theoretical frameworks and real-world deployment.
While GPUs performance has been increasing in the last years, causing the actual execution time of individual computational kernels to shrink, the time required to launch a kernel from the host has remained steady. 
A vast majority of scientific HPC applications operate iteratively. Simulations relying on time-stepping, such as computational fluid dynamics (CFD), electromagnetic solvers, and particle pushers, execute the same sequence of computations repeatedly.
For these reasons, NVIDIA introduced \texttt{CUDA Graphs}\footnote{https://docs.nvidia.com/cuda/cuda-programming-guide/04-special-topics/cuda-graphs.html} in CUDA 10, released in 2018.
A \texttt{CUDA Graph} groups multiple kernel launches into a single workload, by moving the launching responsibility from the host to the device, hence resolving the host bottleneck.
The mentioned article first introduces a performance model and then proposes a strategy for porting HPC applications to \texttt{CUDA Graphs}.

\section{CUDA Graphs}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------
% Talk about CUDA Graphs and its concepts like nodes and edges
CUDA Graphs provide a paradigm shift in how work is submitted to NVIDIA GPUs. 
Traditionally, the host CPU dispatches operations, such as kernel executions and memory transfers, sequentially to CUDA streams. 
When these individual operations are short-lived, the overhead of the CPU launching each task and synchronizing streams can exceed the actual computation time on the GPU, leaving the device underutilized. 
CUDA Graphs mitigate this by allowing the programmer to define a series of operations and their dependencies in advance, effectively offloading the scheduling burden from the host to the device.

Fundamentally, a \texttt{CUDA Graph} is a directed acyclic graph (DAG) where nodes represent individual operations and edges denote execution dependencies. 
A node can encapsulate various types of work, including compute kernels, memory copies, memory allocations, host functions, or even entire child graphs. 
The directed edges strictly dictate the execution order: a dependent node will only commence execution once all of its upstream dependencies have fully completed. 
This explicit declaration of dependencies exposes the maximum inherent concurrency to the CUDA runtime, allowing independent nodes to execute in parallel on the hardware without requiring complex multi-stream synchronization mechanisms on the host CPU.

The lifecycle of a CUDA Graph typically consists of three distinct phases: definition, instantiation, and execution. 

During the \emph{definition} phase, the topology of the graph is constructed. 
This can be achieved either explicitly via API calls (adding nodes and edges programmatically) or implicitly using stream capture. 
Stream capture provides a highly convenient mechanism for existing applications, as it records a sequence of standard stream-based operations into a graph without requiring a complete rewrite of the codebase.
However, manual creation gives more control over the graph configuration, which is crucial to assess its performance.
For this reason, the article only considers manual graph creation.

Once defined, the graph undergoes \emph{instantiation}. Instantiation is a heavy-weight operation that compiles the graph template into a highly optimized, executable format (\texttt{cudaGraphExec\_t}). During this phase, the CUDA driver performs validation, optimizes the scheduling, and pre-allocates necessary resources. Because instantiation incurs significant overhead, it is typically performed only once during the initialization phase of an application, strictly outside the critical computational loop.

Finally, the instantiated graph enters the \emph{execution} phase. The entire pre-compiled workflow can be launched repeatedly with a single API call (\texttt{cudaGraphLaunch}). This launch mechanism bypasses the traditional per-kernel host overhead, reducing latency and improving throughput for repetitive workloads.

Furthermore, for iterative HPC applications where simulation parameters might change between time steps, CUDA provides efficient update mechanisms: instead of destroying and recreating the graph, which would trigger the costly instantiation phase, developers can update the parameters (such as kernel arguments or grid dimensions) of specific nodes within an existing executable graph. 
This ensures that the application maintains the low-latency benefits of graph execution even as the state of the simulation evolves over time.

\section{The Unrolling Strategy}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------
% Explain the unrolling strategy. This strategy divides the workload across multiple graphs since too small graphs would only add overhead and too big graphs would add a much too big creation overhead. Adopt the mini-batch naming to explain this i.e. a mini-batch is an iteration batch.
Constructing a graph that contains only one or two kernel nodes provides negligible performance benefits, as the operations execute in a simple sequence without parallelization advantages. 
Conversely, mapping every single kernel iteration of the application's lifespan into one massive CUDA Graph would result in an excessively large structure, incurring an unsustainable graph creation overhead.
To resolve this trade-off, the proposed unrolling strategy groups a specific number of consecutive kernel launches into a localized subset, which we will refer to as a mini-batch (termed an "iteration batch" in the original article). This mini-batch is then unrolled within a single, static CUDA Graph. The application's main control loop is subsequently modified to iterate over this newly constructed graph rather than launching individual kernels. This structural approach closely mirrors the concept of loop unrolling utilized in standard compiler optimizations.\footnote{Loop unrolling is a compiler optimization technique that expands the body of a loop to execute multiple iterations within a single loop cycle, hence reducing the loop iterations and the overhead associated with loop control mechanisms (e.g., condition evaluation, branching, and counter updates) and allows to execute the unrolled operations in parallel, if independent. The unrolling mechanism faces a trade-off between binary size and execution speed, since unrolling the whole loop could lead to excessive memory usage.}

The primary challenge in deploying this methodology lies in identifying the optimal mini-batch size. Designing the graph requires a precise balance between the performance gains achieved during execution and the added temporal overhead of graph creation. 
This challenge will be addressed in the next section. A visual comparison between the standard sequential kernel execution and the proposed unrolling strategy is illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:unrolling_vs_no_unrolling}.

\section{Performance Model}
\label{sec:performance_model}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------
% Explain the performance model with clear naming and reproduces illustrations
When using CUDA Graphs inside a program, the overall execution time, \(T\), can be divided into the graph creation time, \(T_C\), and the actual execution time, \(T_E\):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:overall_time}
T = T_C + T_E.
\end{equation}
Suppose we have to execute the same kernel \(I_k\) times. We want to find the optimal mini-batch size, \(S\). The ratio between the two will give us the number of iterations, \(I\), to be created:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:iteration_num}
I = \frac{I_k}{S}.
\end{equation}
The graph creation time, \(T_C\), consists of a fixed graph creation overhead, \(b_C\), and the cost of creating a graph, \(k_C\), multiplied by its size (the minibatch size), \(S\):
\begin{equation}
T_C = k_C \cdot S + b_C.
\end{equation}
The actual execution time, \(T_E\), consists of the first launch time, \(T_l\), the overall time of a single graph, \(T_{eg}\), multiplied by the number of iterations, \(I\), and the time between a graph execution and another, \(T_a\), multiplied by the number of iterations minus 1 (since there is no other graph launch after the last one):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:exec_time}
T_{E} = T_{l} + T_{eg}\cdot I + T_{a}(I-1)
\end{equation}
where \(T_{eg}\) equals the execution time of a single kernel, \(T_k\), times the mini-batch size, \(S\), and the time between a kernel execution (within the same graph) and another, \(T_i\), multiplied by the mini-batch size minus 1 (since there is no other kernel launch inside the graph after the last one): 
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:graph_time}
T_{eg} = T_{k}S + T_{i}(S-1)
\end{equation}
Expanding \(T_{eg}\) in \ref{eq:exec_time} according to \ref{eq:graph_time} and then substituing \ref{eq:iteration_num} into \ref{eq:exec_time}, we obtain:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:exec_time_v2}
T_E = \frac{I_{k}(T_{a}-T_{i})}{S} + I_{k}(T_{k} + T_{i}) - T_{a} + T_{l} 
\end{equation}

Recall that we are interested in finding the optimal value for \(S\). So, we now show that \(S\) is the only variable in \ref{eq:exec_time_v2}.
Since we fix the number of kernel executions, \(I_k\), upfront, it is a constant.
\(T_a\) does not depend on the mini-batch size just like \(T_i\).
The kernel execution time, \(T_k\), is assumed fixed.
Lastly, the launch time of the first graph execution, \(T_l\), does not depend on the mini-batch size.

Hence, we can define two constants \(a=I_{k}(T_{a}-T_{i})\) and \(b=I_{k}(T_{k} + T_{i}) - T_{a} + T_{l}\), we can rewrite \ref{eq:exec_time_v2} as:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:exec_time_v3}
T_E = \frac{a}{S} + b
\end{equation}
Since it has been observed that (when not using CUDA Graphs) the time between one kernel execution and another, \(T_b\) is close to \(T_a\), we can see \(a\) as the time we gain between a kernel execution and another by using CUDA Graphs. 

For large \(I_k\) we have that 
\[-T_a + T_l \ll I_k(T_k + T_i) \]
and so the term \(-T_a + T_l\) can be ignored.
So, we can see \(b\) as the total execution time of the iterations, excluding the time between an iteration and another.
Hence, \(b\) can be seen as a lower bound on the iterations execution time where all the kernels are part of a single, large graph.

In the results section, we will show the values found for the four parameters by fitting the overall execution time curve obtained by varying the mini-batch size, \(S\). A comprehensive summary of the base symbols used in this model and their meanings is provided in Table \ref{tab:vocabulary}.

\section{Experimental Setup}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------
To evaluate the proposed unrolling strategy, a custom CUDA application and a suite of test scripts were developed\footnote{\url{https://github.com/riccardoregina/CUDA-Graphs}}. The entire experimental setup can be divided into three main components: the core skeleton application, the automation scripts for data extraction, and the curve fitting procedure for performance modeling.

\subsection{Skeleton Application}
The main program serves as a skeleton application that accepts various configuration parameters via command-line arguments. This modular design allows for flexible experimentation across different workload sizes and graph configurations. The supported parameters are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{---num\_threads} (\texttt{-t}): The total number of CUDA threads to launch, which directly defines the grid dimension for the problem size.
    \item \texttt{---inner\_iterations} (\texttt{-i}): The number of iterations executed internally within a single kernel thread, used to arbitrarily increase the arithmetic intensity of individual kernels.
    \item \texttt{---mini\_batch\_size} (\texttt{-k}): The number of kernel nodes encapsulated within a single CUDA Graph (the size of the unrolled mini-batch).
    \item \texttt{---outer\_iterations} (\texttt{-o}): The total number of times the generated graph (or the sequence of standard kernels) is launched.
    \item \texttt{---repetitions} (\texttt{-r}): The number of times the entire experiment is repeated. This is used to compute statistically robust metrics like mean and standard deviation for the execution times.
    \item \texttt{---seed} (\texttt{-s}): The random seed used to initialize the input dataset on the host memory.
    \item \texttt{---use\_graph} (\texttt{-g}): A boolean flag (0 or 1) to toggle between the standard stream-based execution and the optimized CUDA Graph execution.
    % \item \texttt{--output} (\texttt{-f}): The file path where the extracted benchmark metrics are appended in CSV format.
    % \item \texttt{--print} (\texttt{-p}): An optional flag to print the parsed configuration parameters to standard output.
\end{itemize}

The application logic focuses on iteratively executing the kernel. To implement the CUDA Graphs approach, the application explicitly constructs the graph using the CUDA Graph API. The graph is initialized using \texttt{cudaGraphCreate}, followed by a loop that adds the specified number of kernel nodes (\texttt{mini\_batch\_size}) to the graph via \texttt{cudaGraphAddKernelNode}. To maintain the sequential nature of the iterations, each new kernel node is configured to strictly depend on the previously added node. Once the topology is fully defined, the graph is instantiated into an executable format and uploaded to the device.

A synthetic snippet illustrating the core graph creation logic is provided in Figure \ref{fig:graph_creation}.

\begin{figure*}[htbp]
\begin{lstlisting}[language=C++]
cudaGraphCreate(&graph, 0);
cudaGraphNode_t prev_node = nullptr;

for (int i = 0; i < mini_batch_size; ++i) {
    cudaGraphNode_t current_node;
    // ... set up kernelParams ...
    
    std::vector<cudaGraphNode_t> deps;
    if (prev_node != nullptr) {
        deps.push_back(prev_node);
    }
    
    cudaGraphAddKernelNode(&current_node, graph, 
                           deps.empty() ? nullptr : deps.data(), 
                           deps.size(), &kernelParams);
    prev_node = current_node;
}

cudaGraphInstantiate(&graphExec, graph, NULL, NULL, 0);
cudaGraphUpload(graphExec, 0);
\end{lstlisting}
\caption{Synthetic snippet illustrating the core graph creation logic with the CUDA API.}
\label{fig:graph_creation}
\end{figure*}

\subsection{Automation Scripts}
To extract the performance data systematically, a dedicated suite of bash scripts was developed. These scripts automate the execution of the skeleton application over a sweeping range of parameters, ensuring reproducible and consistent measurements. The main scripts used for the experimental evaluation are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{run\_increase\_nodes.sh}: This script is designed to measure the overhead of graph creation and execution depending on its size. It progressively increases the number of nodes (the mini-batch size) while keeping the total computational work constant, iterating across different thread counts to evaluate the scaling behavior.
    \item \texttt{run\_increase\_iterations.sh}: This script focuses on evaluating the overall speed-up provided by the unrolling strategy. Fixing the mini-batch size to an optimal value, it scales the number of total iterations and executes both the graph-enabled and the baseline versions of the application to gather comparative data.
    \item \texttt{profiler.sh}: This script instruments the application using NVIDIA Nsight Systems (\texttt{nsys}). It captures the hardware-level timeline of the GPU activity, which is crucial for visually verifying that the kernel batching and the graph dependencies are correctly enforced by the CUDA runtime.
\end{itemize}
These scripts collect the execution and creation times for each configuration and store the output in structured CSV files for the subsequent analysis.

\subsection{Performance Modeling and Curve Fitting}
Regarding the data analysis, the collected timings were used to comprehensively validate the proposed performance model. At a conceptual level, we performed a non-linear curve fitting procedure not only on the graph execution times but also on the graph creation times against the varying mini-batch sizes. 

This mathematical approach allowed us to accurately identify the optimal constants for the respective model equations described previously. Specifically, for the execution time $T_E$, the fitting process determines the parameters $a$ (which represents the accumulated time gained between kernel executions, as defined in Equation \ref{eq:exec_time_v3}) and $b$ (the theoretical lower bound of the execution time). For the graph creation time $T_C$, the fitting identifies the constant time per node, $k_C$, and the fixed baseline overhead, $b_C$. 
Once these fitting parameters were successfully extracted from the independent fits of creation and execution times, they were combined and used to derive the function for the total execution time ($T = T_C + T_E$), strictly following the overarching theoretical performance model.

Finally, by evaluating these modeled functions, we derived and compared two distinct optimal sizes for the mini-batch. The theoretical ideal value is mathematically computed by finding the global minimum of the total time function. This is achieved by setting the first derivative of the total time $T$ with respect to the mini-batch size $S$ equal to zero:
\[ \frac{dT}{dS} = k_C - \frac{a}{S^2} = 0 \implies S_{opt} = \sqrt{\frac{a}{k_C}} \]
where $a$ is the execution time gain parameter and $k_C$ is the per-node creation cost. This theoretically calculated value was then compared to the actual real optimal value, which results directly from the minimum points of the empirical data. This comparison effectively verified the consistency of the model's predictions against the observed hardware behavior.

\subsection{Hardware and Software Configurations}
The benchmarking and profiling procedures were conducted across different hardware platforms to ensure the robustness and generalizability of the results. Specifically, the following three configurations were utilized:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Configuration A}: An NVIDIA GeForce RTX 3070 GPU equipped with 8~GB of VRAM. The system utilized NVIDIA Display Driver version 610.43.02 and CUDA version 13.3.
    \item \textbf{Configuration B}: An NVIDIA GeForce GTX 1060 GPU equipped with 6~GB of VRAM. This system utilized NVIDIA Driver version 580.142. Code compilation was performed using CUDA Toolkit version 12.6, running on a driver supporting up to CUDA API version 13.0.
    \item \textbf{Configuration C}: An NVIDIA Tesla T4 GPU equipped with 16~GB of VRAM. The environment was configured with NVIDIA Driver version 580.82.07 and CUDA version 13.0.
\end{itemize}
\textbf{Configuration A} and \textbf{Configuration B} correspond to the personal computing systems of the authors, whereas \textbf{Configuration C} was provisioned using the Google Colab cloud computing platform.
To ensure that the benchmarking results on the personal computers were not distorted by background display rendering tasks, the graphical user interface was explicitly disabled. This was achieved by executing the \texttt{sudo systemctl isolate multi-user.target} command prior to testing, thereby dedicating the GPUs exclusively to the application workloads.

\section{Results}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------
In this section, we present the empirical results obtained from our benchmark and critically compare them against the findings of the original publication. While the qualitative behavior described by the authors is largely confirmed, we observed significant quantitative divergences, particularly regarding the overall speed-up, which warrant a detailed analysis.

\subsection{Execution Trace Analysis}

To validate the timing assumptions underpinning our performance model, we employed NVIDIA Nsight Systems to capture a hardware-level execution trace of the application, illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:profiler}.

This visual evidence substantiates the core assumption \(T_i < T_a\), which was mathematically formalized in Section \ref{sec:performance_model} to explain the theoretical time gain achieved by the unrolling strategy.

Consistent with the original paper, our results (Figure \ref{fig:creation_time}) demonstrate that the graph creation time grows linearly with respect to the mini-batch size (the number of nodes). While the authors observed this linearity up to approximately 2,500 nodes, our tests confirm that this linear trend is strictly maintained even for significantly larger graphs, reaching up to 10,000 nodes. 

Regarding memory consumption, the authors noted a linear increase corresponding to the number of nodes in the graph. Our data exhibits the exact same trend. However, the absolute memory footprint in our tests (approximately 300~MiB for 10,000 nodes at \(10^7\) threads for \textbf{Configuration A}) is proportionally lower than the values reported in the paper (which reached 700-800~MiB at only 2,500 nodes). This discrepancy suggests that the tested version of the CUDA runtime manages graph nodes more efficiently.

Finally, the paper highlights that heavier workloads (such as \(10^7\) threads) incur a slightly higher creation time due to the overhead of uploading the instantiated graph to the GPU. 
This is not true for our \textbf{Configuration A} and \textbf{Configuration B} where the creation time seems mostly indipendent from the worrkload. Instead, for \textbf{Configuration C}, the creation time increases with the workload confirming original paper's claim.

The theoretical model predicts that the execution time (depicted in Figure \ref{fig:execution_time}) should rapidly decrease as the graph size increases, eventually converging towards a lower asymptote following a "U-shaped" curve. Our execution time data clearly demonstrates this convergence, successfully validating the overall theoretical model with one exception for \(10^6\) threads workload.
In fact for all of our configurations a noticeable spike in execution time occurs between batch sizes of 30 and 100, drastically deviating from the fitted curve. Performance analysis revealed a non-monotonic behavior for this intermediate workload (\(10^6\) threads). While for small batches (\(S < 16\)) the use of CUDA Graphs drastically reduces the CPU launch overhead, for larger graph sizes (\(S > 20\)) a slight performance degradation is observed. This phenomenon can be attributed to exceeding the internal dispatch threshold of the NVIDIA driver, which changes the graph submission heuristic, introducing architectural micro-latencies that become visible only in regimes of high L2 cache saturation.

Another notable divergence emerges at very large graph dimensions. The original paper points out that for batch sizes exceeding 2,500 nodes, the execution time ceases to follow the theoretical model and begins to rise due to resource exhaustion. Curiously, our implementation appears significantly more resilient: our execution times (especially for the \(10^3\) and \(10^7\) configurations) remain stable and do not exhibit a marked increase even at a 10,000 node batch size.

Looking at our data for the \(10^3\) thread workload (Figure \ref{fig:optimal_size}), the "U-shaped" curve is extremely clear, and the minimum point is located exactly within this predicted range, definitively validating the authors' observation.

Furthermore, a key conclusion of the paper is that optimizing the graph size becomes less critical as the workload itself becomes larger. Our measurements for the \(10^6\) and \(10^7\) configurations demonstrate this perfectly: the execution ratio remains almost flat (equal to 1) across the entire X-axis. This indicates that for computationally heavy kernels, grouping launches into larger or smaller graphs makes negligible difference to the overall computational efficiency.

The most interesting divergence between our reproduction and the original publication concerns the speed-up metric, as illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:speedup}. 

For light workloads, the paper reports a speed-up of over 1.4x (peaking near 1.5x). In stark contrast, our tests demonstrate a dramatically higher speed-up for the light workload (\(10^3\) threads), stabilizing consistently between 2.3x and 2.5x. This implies that on our specific hardware platform and CUDA version, the traditional CPU-side kernel launch overhead is proportionally much slower, or that the CUDA Graph infrastructure is capable of executing nodes with significantly less latency compared to the NVIDIA A100 system tested by the authors.

Conversely, for the heaviest workloads (\(10^6\) and \(10^7\) threads), the authors obtained only a slight speed-up or no slowdown. Our graphs display a completely flat speed-up of exactly 1.0x for these configurations. We reach the "break-even" point, confirming the theoretical expectation: for large workloads, the kernel execution time completely dominates the launch overhead, resulting in neither apparent benefits nor penalties when using CUDA Graphs.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------
In conclusion, our reproduction fully validates the performance model formulated in the paper: the adoption of CUDA Graphs for iterative loops is effective because the transition time to the next node within a graph is strictly lower than the launch time of a new kernel from the host. 

However, our results, particularly the 2.5x speed-up for light workloads, suggest that the tangible benefits of "Kernel Batching" are heavily dependent on the execution environment (e.g., host CPU, PCIe driver, and GPU architecture). If the host machine suffers from higher CPU-to-GPU latency than the high-end hardware used by the authors, bypassing the CPU via CUDA Graphs can yield performance gains that are substantially higher than those originally published.
